# Material Roles

Classification tells you what something *is about*.

Material role tells you what it *is doing* in the archive.

Without this distinction, exports, drafts, and references all end up looking equally important, which they are not.

---

## Purpose

Material roles add a second axis of understanding:

- Anchor → subject
- Role → function in the archive

---

## Core roles

### source
The origin of thought, design, or data.

Examples:
- original documents
- primary datasets
- authored text
- master design files

---

### process
Intermediate work capturing development.

Examples:
- sketches
- drafts
- test outputs
- lab notes

---

### reference
External or supporting material.

Examples:
- papers
- manuals
- inspiration images

---

### output
Finalised or exported artefacts.

Examples:
- PDFs of finished work
- rendered images
- published versions

---

### residue
Low-value leftovers.

Examples:
- redundant exports
- temporary files
- noise artefacts

---

### duplicate-candidate
Items suspected to be duplicates or versions.

---

## Rules

- every item may have one material role
- roles do not replace anchors
- roles help prioritise organisation decisions

---

## Why this matters

Without roles:
- a draft and a final export look equal
- a reference PDF looks equal to original work

With roles:
- hierarchy becomes visible
- decisions become easier

---

## Practical effect

When organising:
- prioritise **source** and **process**
- group **reference** separately
- compress or archive **output** where appropriate
- remove or ignore **residue**

---

## Short version

Not everything that exists deserves equal attention.

This is the layer that makes that explicit.
