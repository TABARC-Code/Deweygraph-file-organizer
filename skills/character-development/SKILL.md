# Character Development

## Overview

Shallow characters break stories. Every character who matters needs a complete inner life before they enter a scene.

**Core principle:** ALWAYS know who a character IS before you decide what they DO.

**Violating the letter of this process is violating the spirit of character work.**

## The Iron Law

```
NO SIGNIFICANT CHARACTER ENTERS A SCENE WITHOUT A COMPLETE PROFILE
```

If you haven't completed Phase 1, you cannot write a character into a scene.

## When to Use

Use for ANY character who:
- Speaks more than once
- Makes a decision that affects the plot
- Has a relationship with the protagonist
- Is intended to be remembered by the reader

**Use this ESPECIALLY when:**
- A character "just isn't working" in scenes
- The author says a character "feels flat"
- A character behaves differently in different chapters without reason
- A character exists to serve a plot function rather than feeling like a person

## The Four Phases

You MUST complete each phase before proceeding to the next.

### Phase 1: Core Identity

**BEFORE writing a character into any scene:**

1. **Establish their want**
   - What does this character consciously pursue in the story?
   - This is what they would say they want if asked
   - It must be specific: not "love" but "for her father to be proud of her"

2. **Establish their need**
   - What does this character actually require to become whole?
   - This is what the story is teaching them — they may never consciously want it
   - The gap between want and need is where character arcs live

3. **Establish their wound**
   - What formative experience shaped their worldview?
   - This is the emotional backstory that makes their current behavior make sense
   - Wounds create fears, fears create behavior patterns, behavior patterns create character

4. **Establish their lie**
   - What false belief do they hold about themselves or the world as a result of their wound?
   - Characters act on their lie until the story forces them to confront it
   - Example: "I can only rely on myself" / "I don't deserve love" / "Power protects you"

5. **Establish their voice**
   - How do they speak? (Vocabulary, rhythm, what they notice, what they leave unsaid)
   - What would they never say, and why?
   - Write 3-5 sample lines of their dialogue to establish the voice before drafting

### Phase 2: Relational Mapping

**Find the pattern before writing scenes:**

1. **Map their primary relationships**
   - How do they relate to the protagonist? (Ally, antagonist, mentor, foil, mirror)
   - What do they want FROM each relationship?
   - What are they afraid each relationship will reveal about them?

2. **Find the contradiction**
   - Every compelling character contradicts themselves in at least one meaningful way
   - Examples: The brutal warrior who collects pressed flowers. The timid accountant who lies fluently.
   - The contradiction should flow from the gap between their wound and their want

3. **Establish their role in theme**
   - What aspect of the story's central question does this character embody?
   - Are they arguing for the theme, against it, or complicating it?

### Phase 3: Arc Design

**Plan their transformation (or conscious refusal to transform):**

1. **Where do they start?**
   - What is their lie at the beginning of the story?
   - How does their wound shape their behavior in Act 1?

2. **What breaks them open?**
   - What event forces them to confront their lie?
   - What is the worst version of their fear that the story puts in front of them?

3. **Where do they end?**
   - Have they accepted their need? (Positive arc)
   - Have they doubled down on their lie with worse consequences? (Negative arc)
   - Have they remained unchanged while the world changes around them? (Flat arc — valid for certain protagonists)

4. **Track the arc through scenes**
   - Map which chapters show each stage of their transformation
   - Identify the scene where the arc turn happens

### Phase 4: Character Profile Document

Write the validated character profile to `docs/novel-superpowers/characters/[character-name]-profile.md`:

```markdown
# [Character Name]

**Role:** [Protagonist / Antagonist / Mentor / Supporting / etc.]
**First Appears:** [Chapter/Scene reference]

## Core Identity
- **Want:** [What they consciously pursue]
- **Need:** [What they actually require]
- **Wound:** [Formative experience that shaped them]
- **Lie:** [False belief they hold as a result]

## Voice
[2-3 sentences describing how they speak]

Sample dialogue:
> "[Line 1]"
> "[Line 2]"
> "[Line 3]"

## Relationships
- **[Character]:** [Nature of relationship and what they want from it]

## Arc
- **Start:** [Lie and behavior at story's opening]
- **Turning Point:** [Scene/event that breaks them open]
- **End:** [Where they land — acceptance, regression, or stasis]

## Contradiction
[The meaningful contradiction that makes them feel real]

## Thematic Role
[What aspect of the story's central question they embody]
```

## Red Flags — STOP and Fill the Profile

If you catch yourself:
- Writing a character's dialogue without knowing their lie
- Having a character act in a way that serves the plot but contradicts their want
- Describing a character as "mysterious" when you mean "undefined"
- Writing a character who exists only to deliver information to the protagonist
- Thinking "I'll figure out their backstory later"
- Using physical description as a substitute for inner life

**All of these mean: STOP. Return to Phase 1.**

## Common Rationalizations

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "They're a minor character" | Minor characters with complete profiles feel real. Minor characters without them feel like props. |
| "I'll develop them as I write" | Organic development works when built on a foundation. Improvising without a foundation = inconsistency. |
| "The plot needs them to do X" | Characters should do things because of who they are, not because the plot requires it. If their profile won't support X, change the plot or change the profile — don't ignore the conflict. |
| "Their motivation is obvious" | Obvious to you ≠ written on the page. The profile ensures it gets written. |
| "I know this character intuitively" | Intuition must be externalized to be consistent across a long manuscript. |

## When a Character Isn't Working

**Symptoms:**
- Scenes with this character feel mechanical
- The author can't predict how the character would react to a new situation
- The character behaves differently in different chapters for no story reason
- Readers (or editors) say the character "doesn't feel real"

**Return to Phase 1.** The profile is incomplete or incorrect. Find the want, need, wound, and lie — then re-read the existing scenes. The problem will usually be visible.
