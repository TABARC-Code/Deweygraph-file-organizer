# Prose Craft

## Overview

Good prose is invisible. The reader experiences the story, not the sentences. Bad prose makes the reader aware of the writing.

**Core principle:** Every craft choice serves the story's emotional and thematic goals — nothing is purely decorative.

## Voice

Voice is the cumulative effect of all prose choices: diction, syntax, rhythm, what gets noticed and what doesn't.

**Establishing voice before drafting:**
1. Read 3-5 pages of a reference author whose voice fits the project's needs
2. Identify their sentence length patterns, their diction level, their use of interiority vs. external action
3. Write 2-3 sample paragraphs in that register before beginning the draft
4. Record voice notes in the story bible: "Sentences under 20 words. Anglo-Saxon vocabulary. Present tense interiority."

**Voice consistency rules:**
- The narrative voice must be consistent with the POV character's consciousness
- Vocabulary, metaphor, and what gets noticed should be things this character would naturally notice
- Third-person omniscient is the exception: the narrator voice is distinct from the characters
- Check for voice drift in long manuscripts: early chapters establish voice; late chapters must match

**Voice drift warning signs:**
- Vocabulary that doesn't fit the character (philosopher's language in a child's POV)
- Metaphors drawn from contexts the character doesn't inhabit
- Sudden shift in sentence rhythm
- Narrator commenting on things the POV character wouldn't observe

## Dialogue

**Dialogue functions:** Reveals character, advances plot, establishes subtext, creates conflict.

**Rules:**
- Every line of dialogue must do at least one of these jobs
- The subtext matters more than the text: what are characters NOT saying?
- Dialogue that delivers plot information without conflict sounds like exposition ("As you know, Bob...")
- Read dialogue aloud — the ear catches what the eye misses

**Tags and beats:**
- "Said" is invisible. Use it freely.
- Action beats (beats without "said") are stronger than dialogue tags: they ground the dialogue in physical reality
- Avoid adverb tags: `"I love you," he said softly` → `He touched her wrist. "I love you."`

**Subtext:**
```
FLAT (no subtext):
"Are you angry at me?"
"Yes, I'm angry at you."

TEXTURED (subtext):
"Are you angry at me?"
She folded the letter twice, then once more. "Why would I be angry at you?"
```

**Dialect and accent:** Suggest, don't transcribe. Indicate an accent with one or two consistent markers; don't phonetically spell every word — it's exhausting to read.

## Pacing

Pacing is the reader's sense of time passing.

**Techniques:**

| To slow down | To speed up |
|---|---|
| Longer sentences and paragraphs | Short sentences. Fragments. |
| Sensory detail and interiority | Minimal description |
| Reflection on meaning | Immediate action |
| Complex syntax | Simple syntax |
| Scene-level time dilation | Summary and ellipsis |

**The scene-sequel rhythm:**

Every dramatic scene should be followed by a sequel (reaction, emotion, decision) before the next scene begins. Skipping sequels makes the story feel rushed and characters feel robotic. Over-relying on sequels makes it slow.

```
Scene: Protagonist discovers her mentor betrayed her
Sequel (reaction → emotion → thought → decision):
  - Reaction: She goes still. Can't speak.
  - Emotion: First disbelief, then a grief that empties her out.
  - Thought: Everything she built was built on a lie.
  - Decision: She takes the letter and walks out without saying goodbye.
Next scene: Three months later, at the border.
```

## Show vs. Tell

**Tell:** "She was nervous."

**Show:** "She checked the door twice before sitting. When the server asked for her order, she said 'Fine, thank you.'"

**The rule:** Show emotional states through physical action, sensation, and dialogue. Tell when the story needs to move quickly or when the emotion is minor.

**When telling is correct:**
- Transitional moments between scenes
- Minor characters' minor reactions
- When the emotion is already established and needs only brief confirmation
- When the information is factual, not emotional: "Three years passed."

## Description

**Ground in the POV character's perception:** What would this person notice, and how would they describe it? A chef notices different things in a kitchen than a detective.

**Engage multiple senses:** Not just sight. Sound, smell, texture, temperature, and proprioception make scenes feel inhabitable.

**Concrete over abstract:** "A smell of burning rubber and overheated steel" is more vivid than "an industrial smell."

**The telling detail:** One precise, specific detail often creates more atmosphere than three general ones.
- Weak: "The house was old and in disrepair."
- Strong: "A strip of wallpaper had peeled away from the corner and hung down like a gray tongue."

**Description length:** Scale to importance. A significant location the story returns to earns a full paragraph. A corridor the protagonist walks through earns a sentence.

## Common Prose Failures

| Failure | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Overwriting | "The lambent glow of the dying ember cast its penumbral shadow across the weathered cheek of her countenance" | "The last ember threw a dim light across her face." |
| Passive voice | "The door was opened by him" | "He opened the door." |
| Throat-clearing | "She thought to herself that it was strange that..." | "It was strange that..." |
| Filter language | "She saw the light flicker" | "The light flickered." |
| Adverb crutches | "He ran quickly" | "He sprinted" |
| Telling emotion | "He was heartbroken" | "He put the photograph face-down." |
| Head-hopping | Switching POV within a scene without a break | Choose one POV per scene |
| Repetition of sentence structure | Seven sentences in a row beginning with "She" | Vary sentence length and opening |

## Prose Craft Review Process

Before submitting any chapter for review:

1. **Read aloud** — mark every sentence that made you stumble
2. **Check filter words** — "She noticed," "She saw," "She felt," "She thought" — cut them and let the reader observe directly
3. **Check passive voice** — flag every "was [verb]ed" and revise unless passive is deliberate
4. **Check adverbs** — replace with stronger verbs or cut
5. **Check dialogue tags** — convert weak adverb tags to action beats
6. **Check for voice drift** — does the language match the POV character's consciousness?
7. **Check scene-sequel rhythm** — every major scene followed by reaction?

## Prose Craft for Nonfiction

For biography, memoir, self-help, and textbooks, prose craft still matters:

- **Clarity over elegance** — the reader's understanding is always the goal
- **Active voice** — self-help and instructional prose must be direct
- **Concrete examples** — abstract principles always need concrete illustration
- **Paragraph discipline** — one idea per paragraph; topic sentence, support, transition
- **Reader address** — in self-help and workbooks, "you" is appropriate and builds connection; in biography, avoid it
- **Sentence length variation** — even instructional prose benefits from rhythm
