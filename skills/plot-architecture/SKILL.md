# Plot Architecture

## Overview

Random scenes waste the reader's time and create stories that don't land. Structure is not a cage — it's the skeleton that makes movement possible.

**Core principle:** ALWAYS know why a scene exists before you write it. Every scene must change the story's direction or the reader's understanding.

**Violating the letter of this process is violating the spirit of plotting.**

## The Iron Law

```
NO SCENE WITHOUT A CLEAR NARRATIVE FUNCTION
```

If you cannot state what a scene does to the story, it doesn't belong in the outline.

## Structure Models

Choose the model that fits the story — or combine elements. All models are tools, not rules.

### Three-Act Structure

The foundational western narrative model.

```
ACT 1 (≈25%)
- Opening image: establishes the world and protagonist's lie/wound
- Inciting incident: disrupts the status quo, the story "begins"
- First turning point: protagonist commits to the central conflict (no going back)

ACT 2A (≈25%)
- Rising stakes: protagonist pursues their want, faces escalating obstacles
- Midpoint: a reversal — false victory or false defeat that raises stakes dramatically

ACT 2B (≈25%)
- Darkest escalation: protagonist's lie is tested most severely
- All-is-lost moment: protagonist appears to fail completely; worst fear realized

ACT 3 (≈25%)
- Revelation: protagonist confronts their lie and makes a choice
- Climax: the central conflict resolves
- Denouement: new world order established
```

### Save the Cat Beat Sheet (Blake Snyder)

15 beats for character-driven commercial fiction. Useful for plotting scene-level timing:

1. Opening image (1%)
2. Theme stated (5%)
3. Set-up (1–10%)
4. Catalyst (10%)
5. Debate (10–20%)
6. Break into Act 2 (20%)
7. B Story begins (22%)
8. Fun and Games (20–50%)
9. Midpoint (50%)
10. Bad Guys Close In (50–75%)
11. All Is Lost (75%)
12. Dark Night of the Soul (75–80%)
13. Break into Act 3 (80%)
14. Finale (80–99%)
15. Final image (100%)

### Hero's Journey (Campbell / Vogler)

Mythic structure — excellent for epic fantasy, adventure, and transformation narratives:

1. Ordinary World
2. Call to Adventure
3. Refusal of the Call
4. Meeting the Mentor
5. Crossing the Threshold
6. Tests, Allies, Enemies
7. Approach to the Inmost Cave
8. The Ordeal
9. Reward
10. The Road Back
11. Resurrection
12. Return with the Elixir

### Kishōtenketsu (Japanese 4-act)

Conflict-light structure — excellent for literary fiction, slice-of-life, and stories where revelation replaces confrontation:

1. **Ki (Introduction):** Introduce characters and world
2. **Shō (Development):** Develop the setup without conflict
3. **Ten (Twist):** An unexpected element that recontextualizes what came before
4. **Ketsu (Reconciliation):** The twist and the setup reconcile into meaning

### Nonfiction Arc

For biography, memoir, self-help, and educational works:

- **Opening position:** Where is the reader / subject at the start?
- **Central question:** What is the book trying to answer, teach, or prove?
- **Development:** Evidence, narrative, skill-building, or events that progress toward the answer
- **Revelation / Resolution:** The answer, the lesson, the achieved state
- **New position:** Where does the reader / subject end up?

## Scene Function Test

Every scene must pass at least ONE of these:

| Function | Test | Example |
|----------|------|---------|
| Advances plot | Does something happen that changes what's possible next? | Hero escapes the prison |
| Reveals character | Do we understand a character more deeply? | Hero refuses to escape without the prisoner she hates |
| Establishes stakes | Do we understand what failure means? | We see what the villain does with power |
| Builds world | Do we understand the world's rules in a way that will matter? | We see how the magic costs the user |
| Changes relationship | Does a relationship shift in a meaningful way? | Allies become rivals |
| Plants or pays off | Does this set up something that will matter later, or deliver on something earlier? | The locked box from Chapter 1 is opened |

**If a scene passes none of these tests, cut it or rewrite it until it does.**

## Pacing Principles

- **Vary scene length:** Long scenes slow time; short scenes accelerate it. Use this intentionally.
- **Vary scene tension:** Not every scene should be the same emotional register. Readers need breath between peaks.
- **End scenes on hooks:** End scenes at a decision point, a revelation, or a raised question — not at resolution.
- **Scene-sequel rhythm:** Action scene → reaction scene → decision scene → next action. Skipping the reaction makes stories feel hollow.
- **Rising stakes:** Each act should demand more from the protagonist than the last. Stakes that don't rise create flatness.

## Plot Problem Diagnosis

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| "Nothing is happening" | Too many reaction/sequel scenes in a row | Insert a new complication or revelation |
| "Too much is happening" | Missing reaction/sequel scenes | Add space for characters to process and decide |
| "The ending didn't land" | Climax doesn't connect to the theme | Rewrite climax so protagonist's choice embodies their arc resolution |
| "The villain isn't threatening" | Villain hasn't been shown succeeding | Show the villain win something meaningful before the climax |
| "I don't care about the protagonist" | Want/need gap isn't clear | Strengthen the inciting incident and make the want more immediate |
| "The midpoint feels like nothing" | Midpoint reversal isn't sharp enough | The midpoint must flip the protagonist's situation — false victory becomes real crisis or vice versa |
| "Act 2 drags" | Too many obstacles without escalation | Ensure each obstacle in Act 2 is worse than the last AND reveals something about the protagonist |

```markdown
| Beat | Position | Scene/Chapter | Function |
|------|----------|---------------|----------|
| Opening image | 1% | Chapter 1, Scene 1 | [What it establishes] |
| Inciting incident | 10% | Chapter 2 | [What it disrupts] |

| [Name] | [Lie] | [What tests them] | [Acceptance / Regression] |

## Scene Function Audit

[List any scenes from the outline that need function clarification]
```

## Red Flags — STOP and Interrogate the Structure

If you catch yourself:
- Adding a scene because "something needs to happen here"
- Writing a scene that exists to show the author's research
- Creating a subplot that doesn't connect to the protagonist's arc
- Ending your story at the climax without a denouement
- Planning a midpoint that doesn't reverse the story's direction
- Having your protagonist be passive in the second half of Act 2
- Writing "the hero arrives at X" scenes with no obstacle or revelation

**All of these mean: STOP. Apply the Scene Function Test.**
